Reflections on COVID-19

Elliot Z. Lin

2020年 3月 16日

Day zero of the quarantine.

At last, as if we wanted it to happen, delighting at its arrival with an almost child-like satisfaction, we were to self-quarantine effective midnight. It was half past one in the afternoon when my boss and I digested the news. Earlier we had discussed the rumors surrounding a state-wide lockdown over lunch. In between bites of my frozen chicken taquitos from Trader Joe's smothered in harissa I told him about the rumors my brother had heard from his girlfriend, who herself had heard it from her aunt's friend's wife who knew someone who working in the state capital... you get the idea. It wasn't until someone posted a news article reporting that six Bay Area counties were preparing to issue a "shelter-in-place" order that we realized it was actually happening.

"What does it say about... the lockdown?" my boss asked me as he walked across the room over to my desk, a look of concern on his face.

I pulled up the news article on one of my monitors, enlarged the text, and skimmed the text out loud. There: they were announcing it at 1 p.m. It was already a quarter past. I opened a new tab and went to our county's website. Sure enough the full text of the order was already posted.

"Yeah I think we have to stay home now. I don't think we count as "essential business."

As I read that I could almost see a look of sadness wash over his face. How could he bear three long weeks without setting foot in his beloved office?

A Storm is Coming

We had all been thinking about the coronavirus over the past couple of weeks. If we hadn't caught this super-flu yet we had certainly contracted it in our minds. If people weren't discussing their beefed-up hygienic regime they were commenting on light traffic was on the streets.

I don't know who heard it first in the states, but I suspect Asian Americans like myself would have caught wind of it before anyone else. Chinese New Year was in late January, and many people of Chinese descent with family in the old continent flew back to celebrate and visit relatives. My own brother was among them, and if it wasn't for the fact that I was saving my PTO hours for a trip to Japan I was planning to make in April I would have gone with him. Unfortunately for him and many others, the coronavirus epidemic was just picking up momentum, and soon the other East Asian countries were reporting cases of their own.

My brother canceled the rest of his trip and flew home early. The talk of the town (if it even amounted to that in late January) was the two week incubation period and that was enough to get us excited. His roommate wasn't taking any chances; he had already fled the apartment and moved back in with his parents when my brother came home. My brother was hopping mad when he learned his company was only allowing him one week to work from home before he had to go back in.

I was a bit uneasy about the situation myself. I ride public transit (train) every day to work and come into contact with lots of people, directly and indirectly. With the volume of riders going to and coming from San Francisco, someone was bound to have been exposed. On top of that there was the regular flu to worry about. I started wearing a mask on the train just to convince myself I was doing something about it. A few other people on the trains wore masks, but I never saw more than three or four in my car.

In the meantime my relatives in Asia were calling me telling me to stock up on masks because they couldn't find any in their stores. For a couple of weeks I politely ignored their pleas.

"Oh it's not so bad. I'll do it this weekend when I find time."

I never managed to "find time," but when we started seeing fatal cases, I began to worry a little. Maybe I don't have enough to last. I should try and get some before people start cleaning the shelves. On Saturday, February 22, I drove to all the hardware stores and pharmacies in town. All the masks were cleared out.

The Panic of 2020

On February 26, Ash Wednesday in the Liturgical Calendar, California confirmed its first case of coronavirus in a Solano County resident who hadn't traveled outside the country recently. This confirmed community spread, although realistically it had likely been spreading for some time already. Given that the Bay Area isn't far from Solano County, we took it pretty hard down here.

The next day I noticed the train being unusually empty on my commute home. I distinctly remember having an empty seat next to me on the entire leg of my trip. And when I glanced behind me I saw that the entire section was empty, save for two other passengers.

On Friday I drove in to work. Ostensibly I was staying off public transit to mitigate exposure, but really I just woke up late that morning. In the middle of the morning my brother gave me a call.

"Elliot, you gotta go shopping today. I went to Costco earlier this week and they were cleared out of water bottles and toilet paper!"

Well then.

I rushed over to Costco after work and found myself in a busier-than-usual warehouse. There were still pallets of water, but they were being cleared out fast. The jumbo-sized Charmin' Ultra Soft toilet paper was gone. All that was left of the paper products were Kirkland brand, and they were being cleared out too. I pulled out my phone and looked for Charmin' Ultra Soft on Amazon. In stock and with 2-day Shipping! Sure I might be paying a little more for my TP, but that's a price I'm willing to pay for my bum. A week later I would find toilet paper of all sorts sold out on Amazon.

The following Monday I paid another visit to Costco to pick up some more items. I saw a sight I never thought I would behold: empty shelves. All the spam and most of the canned food was picked clean; all that remained were a couple pallets of canned tuna. Where the bags of rice were there were now jugs of popcorn and Utt's Pub Mix. It didn't occur to me that they were out of dried pasta as well. I figured they were long gone by now and their vacant lots replaced with other items. The back section where the stacks of bottled water and paper towels stood were now bare, with nary a Costco associate milling about and a few hapless shoppers gawking in shock. That evening I had walked out with only a jug of maple syrup in hand. Thankfully the local grocery store by my house still carried boxes of dried pasta and pancake mix.

The Road Less Traveled

For the following two weeks I found my mood considerably lifted. Not to be flippant about a global pandemic, but the combination of the flexibility given to my schedule through driving to work and the decrease in traffic as more commuters stayed home meant I got to look forward to a few good episodes of my favorite podcasts every morning when I drove in to work. For once I found beauty on the freeways of the Bay Area. I walked with a spring in my step (for the few paces to and from my car, that is) and I froliced into the office every morning as I enjoyed an extra half hour of peace and quiet to myself due to the fact that my new commute was faster than it had been when I took the train.

Day Zero

It was drizzling as I drove in to work on this Monday morning. Ever since Santa Clara County declared its first confirmed case of COVID-19, affectionately known as the "coronavirus" among us lay people, or more flippantly the "beer flu," I had been driving up to the office. Normally I took the train to work, sharing my seat with 400,000 other riders across the Bay Area.
